## **Goal Tree: Second Renaissance**

### **GOAL**

**Co-initiate a Second Renaissance: a conscious transformation of foundational views, values, practices, and institutions toward a wiser, weller, regenerative civilization.**

This goal is grounded in the whitepapers’ framing of the Second Renaissance as a response to civilizational crisis, rooted in the decline of modernity and the need for conscious participation in shaping a next paradigm.

---

## **Critical Success Factors** **CSF 1 — Shared Diagnosis of the Civilizational Crisis**

The movement must help people understand that today’s crises are not isolated “cracks” but symptoms of deeper foundations: modernity’s inherited views and values.

**Necessary Conditions**

1.1. The crisis is framed as interconnected: ecological breakdown, inequality, authoritarianism, social fragmentation, AI risk, and meaning crisis are treated as mutually reinforcing symptoms.

1.2. The movement identifies modernity’s decaying assumptions without rejecting its gifts.

1.3. Foundational **views and values** are made visible, because they shape perception, behavior, norms, and institutions while often appearing simply as “the way things are.”

1.4. The movement avoids superficial symptom-fixing and directs attention to root causes.

1.5. The diagnosis is emotionally and culturally accessible, not merely intellectual.

---

## **CSF 2 — A Compelling Vision of the Next Paradigm**

The Second Renaissance must offer an attractive “pull factor”: a desirable future strong enough to motivate commitment, imagination, and experimentation.

**Necessary Conditions**

2.1. The future is articulated as an **awakening society**: wiser, weller, life-centered, and oriented toward human and planetary flourishing.

2.2. The vision integrates **inner growth and wisdom**, not only outer institutional reform.

2.3. The vision is grounded in **interbeing**: recognition of profound interdependence among self, others, society, and the living world.

2.4. Art, imagination, spirituality, creativity, and ritual are treated as core civilizational capacities, not optional decoration.

2.5. The new paradigm preserves the best of modernity: reason, freedom, equality, democracy, science, and human dignity, while transcending reductionism, extraction, alienation, and limitless-growth logic.

---

## **CSF 3 — Embodied Practices and Inner Development**

New ideas must become new ways of being. The whitepaper explicitly warns of a knowledge-action gap: intellectual acceptance does not automatically become behavior, culture, or institutions.

**Necessary Conditions**

3.1. Participants cultivate psycho-social capacities: self-awareness, emotional maturity, compassion, discernment, shadow work, conflict capacity, and developmental humility.

3.2. Practices support “waking up, cleaning up, growing up, and showing up.”

3.3. Communities develop shared rituals, reflection practices, learning cycles, and relational norms.

3.4. Inner work is linked to outer action, so spiritual or developmental practice does not become private self-optimization.

3.5. The movement builds skill in disagreement, grief, uncertainty, and non-dogmatic inquiry.

---

## **CSF 4 — Pockets of Coherent New Culture**

Paradigm shifts begin in **pockets**: small, dense groups practicing new ways of living, thinking, and acting together.

**Necessary Conditions**

4.1. Pockets form as intentional communities, co-living spaces, neighborhoods, enterprises, schools, online communities, or local civic experiments.

4.2. Members share enough commitment and interaction density to make the new culture livable.

4.3. Pockets function as laboratories for new social forms, not as isolated retreats.

4.4. Pockets produce thought leadership, practical models, stories, art, and examples that can travel beyond the group.

4.5. Pockets are supported by resources, patronage, infrastructure, and governance capacities.

4.6. Pockets learn from failure and document what works.

---

## **CSF 5 — Sangha: A Wider Community of Practice**

Pockets need a supportive surrounding ecology: people who may not live full-time in the new paradigm but actively learn, practice, support, and normalize it.

**Necessary Conditions**

5.1. A broader community engages with the ideas, practices, events, and cultural outputs of pockets.

5.2. The sangha nourishes pockets with attention, participation, money, skills, legitimacy, and social protection.

5.3. There is a “revolving door” between sangha and pocket, allowing people to experiment with different levels of commitment.

5.4. Practices are accessible for families, workers, elders, youth, and people outside elite intellectual circles.

5.5. The sangha helps translate the Second Renaissance into ordinary life.

---

## **CSF 6 — Movement-Level Coherence and Cultural Transmission**

As pockets and sanghas become aware of each other, they must develop a larger identity, shared story, and movement infrastructure.

**Necessary Conditions**

6.1. Diverse pockets see themselves as part of a larger historical transition.

6.2. Common narratives, values, symbols, language, and principles emerge without becoming rigid ideology.

6.3. Cultural content spreads through writing, art, media, education, gatherings, and public discourse.

6.4. The movement remains pluralistic and globally rooted, avoiding a narrowly Eurocentric or totalizing frame.

6.5. Movement-building increases mainstream awareness and participation.

6.6. The movement develops the ability to influence institutions, policy, economics, education, and governance.

6.7. Shared strategic artifacts are governed as living, revisable commons, with clear processes for challenge, update, stewardship, and provisional adoption.\*\*

---

## **CSF 7 — New Institutions and Regenerative Social Architecture**

A paradigm cannot endure only as belief. It must become embodied in institutions, economic systems, governance forms, education, technology, and daily life.

**Necessary Conditions**

7.1. New social and economic institutions are grounded in human and planetary good rather than financial value alone.

7.2. Institutions reward cooperation, regeneration, wisdom, care, and long-term responsibility.

7.3. Education develops the whole person: cognitive, emotional, ethical, creative, ecological, relational, and spiritual capacities.

7.4. Technology is governed by wisdom, precaution, and life-serving purpose.

7.5. Governance systems support participation, subsidiarity, pluralism, and collective intelligence.

7.6. Economic models move from extraction and accumulation toward sufficiency, reciprocity, and regeneration.

---

## **CSF 8 — Wise Transition Through Collapse and Renewal**

The Second Renaissance must operate during a dangerous “time between worlds,” where decay of the old paradigm creates both risk and possibility.

**Necessary Conditions**

8.1. The movement can hospice what is dying, compost what is no longer viable, and steward what remains valuable.

8.2. It builds resilience against ecological, political, technological, and social shocks.

8.3. It avoids both naive utopianism and cynical collapse fatalism.

8.4. It prepares people psychologically and practically for disruption.

8.5. It turns crisis energy into constructive transition rather than reactionary backlash.

8.6. It builds “pull factors” strong enough to complement the “push factors” of modernity’s decline.

## **1\. What counts as progress?**

Using the goal tree, progress means increasing the presence and quality of the **necessary conditions** for the Second Renaissance.

So a group should measure progress along several levels:

| Level | Progress question | Example indicators |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Inner development** | Are people becoming more capable of wise, non-reactive, life-serving action? | depth of practice, conflict maturity, self-reported wellbeing, peer feedback, capacity to “disagree better,” reduced reactivity |
| **Pocket formation** | Is a coherent new culture becoming livable in a real group? | number of active members, interaction density, shared practices, retention, trust, governance health, quality of experiments |
| **Sangha formation** | Is there a wider community of practice around the pocket? | recurring participants, volunteers, donors, apprentices, event return rate, practice adoption outside the core group |
| **Movement formation** | Are pockets and sanghas linking into a wider identity and shared narrative? | collaborations, shared language, cross-pollination, public recognition, partner networks, invitations, replication |
| **Cultural transmission** | Are new views and values spreading? | people adopting the frame, references by others, content that changes behavior, new groups inspired to act |
| **Institutional influence** | Are new forms entering education, governance, economy, technology, or civil society? | pilots, policies, curricula, organizational adoptions, new institutions, funding flows toward aligned work |
| **Regenerative effects** | Are human and ecological conditions actually improving? | wellbeing, community resilience, ecological restoration, reduced extraction, increased mutual aid, local adaptive capacity |

This fits the Second Renaissance “How” paper: transition begins with **pockets**, expands through **sangha**, and becomes a broader **movement** as groups become aware of one another and develop shared purpose, visibility, participation, and cultural influence.

## **2\. What would count as throughput?**

For this kind of system, I would define throughput as:

> **The rate at which the group generates durable, embodied adoption of wiser views, values, practices, and social forms in people, communities, and institutions.**

Even shorter:

> **Throughput \= verified transformation adopted by others.**

That means the group’s throughput is **not** simply:

* number of events held  
* number of essays published  
* number of followers  
* number of members  
* amount of money raised  
* number of conversations had

Those may matter, but only insofar as they create real movement toward the goal.

A better throughput unit might be something like:

> **A person, group, or institution that adopts and continues practicing a Second Renaissance pattern because of the group’s work.**

So examples of throughput could include:

1. **A person becomes a committed practitioner**, not just a passive consumer.  
2. **A new pocket forms** and sustains itself.  
3. **A sangha deepens** around an existing pocket.  
4. **A practice spreads** beyond the founding group.  
5. **A cultural artifact changes behavior**, not merely gets attention.  
6. **A partner institution adopts a new pattern**: governance, education, ritual, inner development, regenerative economics, community practice.  
7. **A model becomes replicable**, meaning others can use it without the founders present.  
8. **A conflict, governance, or developmental practice becomes embodied** in a group’s operating culture.

So a practical throughput metric could be:

> **Number of verified adoption-events per quarter, weighted by depth, durability, and strategic importance.**

For example:

| Throughput category | Low-quality version | High-quality version |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Individual adoption | Someone attends one event | Someone enters regular practice for 3+ months |
| Community adoption | A group likes the ideas | A group changes its governance, rituals, or norms |
| Pocket formation | A meetup happens once | A stable, dense, practicing group forms |
| Cultural transmission | A post gets views | Another group uses the framework independently |
| Institutional adoption | A leader expresses interest | An organization changes policy, curriculum, or resource allocation |

## **3\. Throughput, inventory, and operating expense for a Second Renaissance group**

Dettmer discusses TOC’s three basic operational measures: **Throughput, Inventory/Investment, and Operating Expense**, including their use beyond conventional businesses and in not-for-profit settings.

Translated into this context:

### **Throughput**

The rate of real, goal-relevant transformation.

Examples:

* committed practitioners formed  
* pockets created  
* sanghas deepened  
* practices adopted  
* institutions influenced  
* cultural patterns replicated

### **Inventory / Investment**

Things the group has built or accumulated but that have not yet become transformation.

Examples:

* unpublished research  
* content library  
* curriculum drafts  
* trained facilitators not yet deployed  
* mailing list  
* donor relationships  
* community goodwill  
* buildings, tools, software  
* partially formed partnerships

Inventory is not bad. It is potential throughput. But if it never converts into adoption, it becomes stagnation.

### **Operating Expense**

The energy spent to keep the system running.

Examples:

* staff time  
* meetings  
* admin  
* rent  
* coordination overhead  
* software  
* fundraising effort  
* emotional labor  
* governance maintenance

The trap is to confuse operating expense with progress. A group can be very busy and produce little throughput.

## **4\. A useful measurement stack**

I would use four kinds of measures at the same time.

### **A. Throughput measures**

These are the primary measures.

Examples:

* number of people entering sustained practice  
* number of active practice groups formed  
* number of new pockets created  
* number of partner communities adopting practices  
* number of institutions implementing a Second Renaissance-inspired pattern  
* number of independent replications  
* number of cultural products that lead to documented behavior change

### **B. Conversion measures**

These show whether activity is turning into throughput.

Examples:

* event attendee → recurring participant  
* recurring participant → practitioner  
* practitioner → contributor  
* contributor → pocket member  
* pocket member → pocket founder  
* reader → sangha member  
* partner conversation → pilot  
* pilot → institutional adoption

These may be among the most important metrics.

### **C. Quality / depth measures**

These prevent shallow growth.

Examples:

* retention after 3, 6, 12 months  
* peer-assessed maturity of practice  
* strength of relationships  
* capacity to handle conflict  
* depth of shared language  
* evidence of changed choices  
* quality of governance  
* degree of felt belonging  
* ecological or social impact of actual projects

### **D. Constraint measures**

These identify what is limiting throughput.

Examples:

* too few trained facilitators  
* weak onboarding  
* unclear narrative  
* insufficient funding  
* poor conflict capacity  
* content not converting into practice  
* pockets too isolated  
* high burnout  
* unclear governance  
* no replicable model

In TOC terms, the group should ask: **what is currently the constraint on the rate of real transformation?**

## **5\. How the LTP helps answer this question**

The Logical Thinking Process helps because it stops the group from measuring what is easiest to count and forces it to reason from the goal.

Dettmer’s Intermediate Objectives Map begins with a clear goal, identifies the critical success factors required to realize it, then identifies necessary conditions beneath those factors. It provides a benchmark for comparing what should be happening with what is actually happening.

Applied here, LTP helps in five ways:

### **1\. It clarifies the system goal**

Without a clear goal, the group may optimize for attention, growth, revenue, or internal harmony. The IO Map asks: **what is the system for?**

For a Second Renaissance group, the goal is not “grow the organization.” It is something like:

> **Increase the rate and quality of conscious transition toward wiser, weller, regenerative culture.**

### **2\. It distinguishes goal, critical success factors, and necessary conditions**

This prevents metric confusion.

For example:

* “Hold events” is not the goal.  
* “Build sangha” may be a critical success factor.  
* “Run recurring gatherings” may be a necessary condition.  
* “Attendance” may be an operating indicator.  
* “Participants entering sustained practice” may be throughput.

### **3\. It exposes undesirable effects**

The group can build a Current Reality Tree around symptoms like:

* many people attend but few commit  
* content is appreciated but not embodied  
* founders are overloaded  
* pockets do not replicate  
* conflict causes fragmentation  
* people agree intellectually but do not change behavior  
* mainstream audiences find the language inaccessible

Then it can identify the root causes that constrain throughput.

### **4\. It identifies the constraint**

The group should not improve everything at once. It should find the bottleneck limiting transformation.

For example, the constraint might be:

* not enough facilitators  
* weak onboarding pathway  
* unclear practice architecture  
* lack of shared language  
* lack of credible examples  
* lack of local containers  
* lack of money  
* lack of conflict capacity

Throughput improves most when the constraint improves.

### **5\. It links strategy to action**

The Future Reality Tree can test whether proposed interventions would actually produce desired effects. The Prerequisite Tree can identify obstacles. The Transition Tree can sequence actions. Dettmer presents these tools as part of an integrated thinking process: IO Map, Current Reality Tree, Evaporating Cloud, Future Reality Tree, Prerequisite Tree, and Transition Tree.

## **6\. A concrete scorecard**

A group could review this monthly:

| Metric | Why it matters |
| ----- | ----- |
| **New sustained practitioners** | Core individual-level throughput |
| **Practitioner retention** | Depth and durability |
| **New pocket experiments launched** | Cultural embodiment |
| **Pockets still active after 6 months** | Durability of new culture |
| **Sangha participation rate** | Supportive ecology |
| **Conversion from event/content to practice** | Whether transmission works |
| **Independent replications** | Whether the model travels |
| **Institutional pilots/adoptions** | Structural influence |
| **Constraint identified this month** | Focus |
| **Constraint improved this month?** | Whether effort increases throughput |
| **Burnout / trust / conflict health** | System viability |

A good north-star metric might be:

> **Durable adoption-events per quarter.**

Then the group can define adoption-events at different weights:

| Adoption-event | Weight |
| ----- | ----- |
| Person joins one recurring practice | 1 |
| Person sustains practice for 3 months | 3 |
| Person becomes a contributor/facilitator | 5 |
| New sangha circle forms | 8 |
| New pocket forms | 13 |
| Pocket survives 6 months | 21 |
| External group replicates a practice | 21 |
| Institution adopts a practice or model | 34 |

The exact weights matter less than the discipline: **measure transformation, not activity.**

## **Bottom line**

For a Second Renaissance group, **throughput is the rate of durable cultural transformation**: people, groups, and institutions adopting wiser views, values, practices, and forms of life.

The LTP helps by making the group reason backward from the goal, identify the necessary conditions, distinguish activity from throughput, find the current constraint, and design interventions that actually increase the rate of meaningful transition.
