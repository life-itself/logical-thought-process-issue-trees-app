# Why I've Wanted This Tool for Over a Decade

I have wanted this tool for more than ten years. Long enough that I almost built it myself, three or four years ago, and then didn't. This is the story of why — and why I think its time has finally come.

## Arguing past each other

For as long as I can remember I have been bothered by how badly we argue.

Not the heat of it — the structure of it. People talk past each other. Claims are never made specific enough to actually engage with. I would read a two-hundred-page book and, at the end, be unable to tell you what the author's central thesis actually was, let alone the sub-claims it rested on or the evidence that was supposed to hold it up.

I came to this early. I did debating at school, where you learn very quickly that an argument has parts — that a case is something you build, not something you feel. And I watched my father argue legal cases. Watching him, I saw what becomes possible when real structure and real resources are brought to bear on a single question: how an argument can be taken apart, tested, and reassembled until what is actually at stake becomes clear.

Two frustrations crystallised out of all this, and they have stayed with me ever since.

The first is **vagueness**. Almost nobody breaks down what they are actually claiming. We gesture at positions instead of stating them. And because the claim is never made precise, it can never really be examined.

The second is **the lack of accumulation**. Even after a long and genuine debate, you can almost never point to a place and say: *we have now settled sub-point X, and that has implication Y for the main thesis.* Evidence arrives, but it is never clear which claim it even bears on. Nothing compounds. Every argument starts again from the beginning.

## Wanting a graph

What I wanted, intuitively, was a **graph**. Some kind of overview that showed the claims and how they related to one another — a map you could stand back from and see the whole shape of a disagreement, and then zoom into the one node where the real work needed to happen.

I didn't have a name for it. I just knew the shape of the thing I was missing.

Then, in my early thirties, I came across **Minto pyramids** and **issue trees**. They were pointing at exactly the same thing I had been reaching for. I found them genuinely, immediately useful. But they were also slow and laborious to construct by hand — enough that, in practice, I mostly didn't. And that is when I first thought: *there should be an app for this.* I got as far as seriously considering building one.

I didn't. Partly timing, partly that the tooling to make it feel effortless simply wasn't there yet.

## Logic trees, and why now

Recently I was shown **logic trees** — the Logical Thinking Process out of the Theory of Constraints. I hadn't encountered them before. They felt like issue trees with more power: not just a decomposition of a claim, but a whole pipeline from goal to problem to solution to the concrete next step. I still want to read more deeply into them. But the recognition was immediate: same instinct, more structure.

And this time something is different. Two things, really.

The first is **AI**. The laborious part — the part that always defeated me and everyone else — is exactly the part that AI is now good at. Decomposing a claim, drafting a first tree, reading a document and proposing where its arguments attach: this used to take hours of disciplined effort. It doesn't have to any more.

The second is the idea I keep coming back to: the tree as a **stable scaffold that discourse accumulates against**. Documents come and go. Forum threads scroll away. But if there is a persistent structure that new material gets mapped onto — this quote supports that node, this study complicates this branch — then discussion stops being disposable. It starts to compound. Cathedral-building instead of noise.

## The honest part

I should be honest that the hardest part of this was never the technology, and it still isn't.

The real barrier to structured thinking has always been that it is *confronting*. It takes discipline. It forces you to decide what actually matters and what doesn't. And, most of all, it tends to expose misalignment — the moment a group gets precise about its goal is the moment it discovers it never really agreed on one. Staying vague is comfortable. Getting clear is not.

I have felt all of this myself, including in organisations I have run. So I don't imagine a tool makes the discomfort disappear. But I do believe that if we can make the *disciplined* part dramatically cheaper — if getting to a good tree takes minutes of AI-assisted work rather than a lost afternoon — then far more people will be willing to pay the smaller psychological price that remains.

For a long time I waited for someone else to build this. I have now stopped waiting. Together with [David Joseph](https://www.promise.foundation/how-it-works) I have started, and the first rough pieces already exist — you can [explore an early demo of the claim-tree and annotation idea here](../claim-tree-annotation-demo/index.html).

If any of this resonates with a frustration you have felt too, I would love to hear from you.
