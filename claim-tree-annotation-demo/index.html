Issue Tree + Cumulative Annotation

Step 1 — The question
Does remote work increase productivity?

We start with a question, not a claim — because it's contested and we want to reason honestly about it. The goal is to decompose it so we can see exactly which sub-questions are in dispute.

↓ scroll
Step 2 — Issue tree

AI decomposes the root question into sub-questions. Each branch isolates a distinct dimension. Answering the root question means answering these.

Does remote work increase productivity?
├── For whom?
│ ├── Individual contributors vs managers?
│ └── Deep work roles vs collaborative roles?
├── For what tasks?
│ ├── Focused / solo tasks?
│ └── Creative / collaborative tasks?
├── Over what time horizon?
│ ├── Short-term (first months)?
│ └── Long-term (1+ years)?
└── Under what conditions?
    ├── Home setup quality?
    └── Managerial practices?
The tree makes clear that "remote work is/isn't productive" is not a single claim. Most arguments in the wild talk past each other because they're answering different sub-questions.

Step 3 — Source document

A relevant paper exists. We feed it to the AI. It reads the document against the tree and identifies which passages speak to which nodes.

Bloom, N., Liang, J., Roberts, J., & Ying, Z. J. (2015)
"Does Working from Home Work? Evidence from a Chinese Experiment"
Quarterly Journal of Economics — Stanford GSB

We report the results of a randomized control trial of working from home among call centre employees at Ctrip, a NASDAQ-listed Chinese travel agency. We find a 13% performance increase among work-from-home employees, with about two thirds due to fewer breaks and sick days, and one third due to more calls per minute attributed to a quieter working environment. → root

A key finding is that the performance improvement is concentrated in solo tasks — call handling — rather than team-based activities. → tasks Employees working from home had significantly lower rates of promotion, suggesting potential long-term career costs.

The study covers call-centre workers only. Results may not generalise to knowledge workers, managers, or creative roles. → for whom

At the end of the experiment, employees were given the choice to return to the office. Roughly half chose to return, and among this group the productivity gain largely reversed, suggesting individual preferences for working environment are heterogeneous and matter for outcomes. → time horizon

Step 4 — Annotated tree

The AI maps each highlighted passage to its node on the tree (see left). Each annotation is tagged — supports, complicates, or limits scope — and links back to the source.

This is one document. The same tree can receive a second paper, a blog post, a comment-thread argument. Each new source gets mapped against the same structure. Over time the tree becomes a structured body of evidence — not a flat list of links, but every piece of evidence located relative to a specific sub-question.

That's the difference between discourse that accumulates and discourse that scatters.
