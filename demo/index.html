Issue Tree + Cumulative Annotation

A walkthrough of the structured sense-making process

Step 1

Start with a question

Everything begins with a question or claim we want to reason about carefully. We use a question because it's more honest — we don't know the answer yet.

Does remote work increase productivity?
Why this question?
💬
Genuinely contested. Employers and employees disagree. Research exists but is often cited selectively. A good issue tree makes the structure of the debate explicit — so we can see exactly which sub-questions are actually in dispute.
Step 2

Decompose into an issue tree

AI breaks the root question into sub-questions. Each branch isolates a distinct dimension of the debate. The answer to the root question depends on answers to these sub-questions.

Does remote work increase productivity?
├── For whom?
│ ├── Individual contributors vs managers?
│ └── Deep work roles vs collaborative roles?
├── For what tasks?
│ ├── Focused / solo tasks?
│ └── Creative / collaborative tasks?
├── Over what time horizon?
│ ├── Short-term (first months)?
│ └── Long-term (1+ years)?
└── Under what conditions?
    ├── Home setup quality?
    └── Managerial practices?
What this gives us
The tree makes clear that "remote work is/isn't productive" is not a single claim. The real disagreements are about which workers, doing which tasks, over what timeframe. Most arguments talk past each other because they're answering different sub-questions.
Step 3

Bring in a source document

Someone has written something relevant — a paper, blog post, news article. We feed it to the AI, which reads it against the tree and identifies which parts speak to which nodes.

Bloom, N., Liang, J., Roberts, J., & Ying, Z. J. (2015).
"Does Working from Home Work? Evidence from a Chinese Experiment." Quarterly Journal of Economics.
Stanford Graduate School of Business — working paper

We report the results of a randomized control trial of working from home among call centre employees at Ctrip, a NASDAQ-listed Chinese travel agency. We find a 13% performance increase among work-from-home employees, with about two thirds of this due to fewer breaks and sick days, and one third due to more calls per minute attributed to the quieter working environment.

A key finding is that the performance improvement is concentrated in solo tasks — call handling — rather than team-based activities. Employees working from home had significantly lower rates of promotion, suggesting potential long-term career costs.

At the end of the experiment, employees who had been assigned to work from home were given the choice to return to the office. Roughly half chose to return, and among this group, the productivity gain largely reversed, suggesting individual preferences for working environment are highly heterogeneous and matter for productivity outcomes.

What happens next
The AI reads this document against the issue tree. It identifies three distinct passages that each speak to different nodes. It proposes annotations — the user reviews and confirms.
Step 4

Annotated tree

The tree now carries evidence. Each annotation is a fragment from the source doc, mapped to the node it speaks to, tagged with whether it supports, complicates, or limits the claim. More documents can be added — the tree accumulates.

Key point
This tree can now receive more documents. Each new paper or article gets mapped onto the same structure. Over time the tree becomes a structured summary of everything known — not a flat list of links, but an organised body of evidence where every piece is located relative to a specific sub-question.
