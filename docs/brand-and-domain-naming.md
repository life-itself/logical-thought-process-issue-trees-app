# Brand and domain naming: ClaimTrees

*Decision memo — 12 July 2026*

## Context: what this product is

This project is not primarily a goal-setting, project-management, or generic diagramming tool. It is a place to make a question or proposition inspectable:

1. state a top-level claim or question;
2. decompose it into the sub-claims or sub-questions on which it depends;
3. map source passages to the relevant nodes as supporting, challenging, or qualifying evidence; and
4. keep that tree as the stable scaffold as more material arrives.

The resulting promise is **cumulative public reasoning**: instead of arguments dissolving into disconnected posts and documents, a shared logical structure and its evidence can improve over time. In short: *build claim trees, map evidence, grow shared understanding.*

This is inspired by Logical Thinking Process (LTP) and issue-tree practice, but it is not a narrow implementation of one LTP instrument. The public brand should describe the job the product actually does, rather than require visitors to know the LTP vocabulary.

## LTP and “claim tree”

LTP's named tools include a Goal Tree (also called an Intermediate Objectives Map), Current Reality Tree, Future Reality Tree, Prerequisite Tree, and, historically, Transition Tree. A Goal Tree defines a desired outcome and the necessary conditions for reaching it. Its core reading is instrumental: **in order to achieve X, we must have Y**.

That is related to, but different from, this product's primary reading: **is claim X justified; what sub-claims does it require; and what evidence bears on each?** Here a tree is an evidential and deliberative scaffold, not principally a strategy map or execution plan.

“Claim tree” is established, intelligible language in neighbouring argumentation and assurance-case practice. Goal Structuring Notation, for example, treats a top-level goal as a claim supported by subclaims and evidence. That overlap supports the name without claiming that “Claim Tree” is formal LTP nomenclature.

The accurate positioning is therefore:

> **ClaimTrees is LTP-inspired, but not an orthodox LTP tool.**

It borrows LTP's commitment to explicit, inspectable logic and applies it to the different problem of shared inquiry and accumulated evidence.

## Recommendation

Use **ClaimTrees** as the single name for the product, website, and brand.

- **Brand/site/product:** ClaimTrees
- **Core object:** a claim tree
- **Suggested line:** “Build claim trees. Map evidence. Grow shared understanding.”
- **Primary domain:** `claimtrees.com`
- **Defensive domain:** `claimtrees.org`, redirected to the primary site

Plural is intentional. It makes the brand a home for many trees and many inquiries, while retaining the simple singular product language: “Create a claim tree.” It also gives the project a slightly more expansive, communal feel than the singular `ClaimTree`.

At the time of this memo, direct registry RDAP lookups returned “not found” for `claimtrees.com` and `claimtrees.org`, a strong availability signal. `claimtree.com` was registered; `claimtree.org` returned “not found.” Availability can change immediately, so confirm and purchase through a registrar/Domainr before treating this as final.

## Naming options

| Option | What it immediately communicates | Strengths | Risks / why not first choice | Assessment |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **ClaimTrees** | A collection of structured claims | Precise fit; supports evidence, rebuttal, and qualification language; memorable; aligns with the actual stable object | Slightly analytic; “claim” can sound adversarial to some audiences | **Recommended** |
| **ClaimTree** | One argument/claim decomposed into parts | The clearest singular product-object name | `claimtree.com` is already registered; singular brand has less of the shared-commons feel | Strong fallback if `.org` is preferred |
| **InquiryTree** | Collaborative exploration of a question | Warm, non-adversarial, public-interest tone | Less explicit about claims and evidence; needs a descriptive tagline | Best alternative if tone matters more than directness |
| **EvidenceTree** | Evidence organised in a tree | Makes source annotation central and easy to understand | Misses the logical/decomposition layer; suggests a document repository | Credible, but narrower than the product |
| **ReasonTree** | Structured reasoning | Elegant and broad | Abstract; sounds philosophical and is less distinctive | Good conceptual alternative |
| **ReasonGrove** | A shared place for reasoning | Warm, distinctive, ownable-feeling; evokes many trees | Needs explanation; less useful for search and first-visit comprehension | Best coined/umbrella-brand alternative |
| **ArgumentTree** | Formal argument structure | Exact meaning for many expert users | “Argument” feels combative; emphasises debate over inquiry | Accurate but poor public tone |
| **GoalTrees** | Hierarchy of desired outcomes | Familiar to LTP practitioners; memorable | Strong existing association with OKRs, KPIs, strategy, task management, and personal productivity; does not foreground evidence | Not recommended for this product |
| **GoldTrees** | A memorable, positive metaphor | Pleasant sound; potentially evocative of durable value | Gives no clue about logic, evidence, or inquiry; more marketing effort required | Better as a programme/metaphor than product name |

## Why not GoalTrees?

The LTP connection is the best argument for it. A Goal Tree is a genuine LTP tool, and the name has a clear internal logic: the tree lays out the necessary conditions for an overarching goal.

For a public product, however, the prevailing meaning is materially different. “Goal tree” is used by goal-setting, performance-management, and KPI products for decomposing objectives and tracking progress. There are also active adjacent products using **GoalTree** for personal goal/task management. A visitor arriving at `goaltrees.org` or hearing the name would reasonably expect planning software, not an environment for evaluating contested propositions and attaching evidence.

This is not just a search-engine concern. It is a product-positioning concern: the name would repeatedly require explanation exactly where the product should be immediately legible.

LTP terminology can still have an important place in the site:

- describe the project as **inspired by LTP, issue trees, and argument mapping**;
- offer a **Goal Tree** mode/template when users are explicitly reasoning about necessary conditions for achieving an aim; and
- explain the relationship in documentation for the LTP-experienced audience.

That honours the lineage without making one specialised instrument name the public category label.

## Decision criteria used

1. **Semantic fit:** does the name describe claims plus evidence, not merely diagrams or planning?
2. **First-visit comprehension:** will an intelligent new visitor have the right expectation before reading a pitch?
3. **Memorability and pronounceability:** can it be said, heard, typed, and recalled without friction?
4. **Distinctiveness:** does it avoid direct confusion with adjacent goal/KPI/task products?
5. **Search and naming flexibility:** can the site naturally use phrases such as “claim tree,” “evidence mapping,” and “structured reasoning”?
6. **Room to grow:** can it hold many topics, documents, and communities without sounding like a one-off tool?

ClaimTrees is the strongest overall match. It is descriptive enough to be understood, distinctive enough to be a real name, and directly connected to the durable scaffold at the centre of the product.
